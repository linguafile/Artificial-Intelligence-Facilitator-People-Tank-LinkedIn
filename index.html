The premise

Capability isn't about the tool. It's about the habits around it.

Anyone can be shown which button to press. The teams that actually get value are the ones who learn how to ask, how to check, and where the tool earns trust — and where it doesn't. That is a teachable skill. It's the one I build a room around.

What a programme delivers

From first contact to power user.

The teachable skill, live

Same tool. Same minute. Very different result.

What most people type
"Write something about our new recycling bins."
A vague, generic paragraph. Right topic, wrong everything else — no audience, no channel, no tone, no length. You spend more time fixing it than you saved.
What I teach people to type
"You're our council comms officer. Write a 90-word notice for residents about the new recycling bins — friendly, plain English, what changes and the date. End with where to ask questions."
A finished, on-tone notice with the right length, a clear call to action, and a sign-off. Usable as-is, or a strong first draft. The difference wasn't the AI. It was the ask.
The habit that makes it stick

It will always sound certain.

Certainty isn't accuracy — and knowing the difference is what makes AI safe to rely on at work.

A confident wrong answer is the single biggest reason AI adoption stalls after the first exciting week. So I teach one habit early: check the things that carry weight. Get that right, and people trust the tool for the right reasons — which is exactly when it starts paying off.
